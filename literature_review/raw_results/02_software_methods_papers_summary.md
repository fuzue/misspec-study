# Software & methods papers for microbial growth-curve fitting

Survey of 18 tools / foundational papers, with emphasis on the *default* uncertainty quantification each delivers to a user. Citation counts are from Semantic Scholar (May 2026 snapshot via the Graph API); they should be read as order-of-magnitude indicators of community uptake.

Companion data table: `02_software_methods_papers.tsv`.

## Overall picture: how the most-used tools handle uncertainty

The single sharpest takeaway is that the **community uses tools that emit point estimates almost by default**, and that of the tools that *do* emit interval-valued output, the overwhelming majority emit **Wald / asymptotic CIs from the NLS information matrix** rather than calibrated intervals.

Grouped by the dominant fit method that the published paper recommends:

- **Nonlinear least squares (NLS) on a parametric primary model — Wald-type SEs/CIs only.** Growthcurver (Sprouffske & Wagner 2016, ~740 citations; DOI 10.1186/s12859-016-1016-7), drc (Ritz et al. 2015, ~3,100 citations; DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0146021), GrowthRates (Hall et al. 2014, ~490 citations; DOI 10.1093/molbev/mst187), DMFit / Baranyi-Roberts (Baranyi & Roberts 1994, ~2,600 citations; DOI 10.1016/0168-1605(94)90157-0), Dashing Growth Curves (Reiter & Vorholt 2024, ~13 citations; DOI 10.1186/s12859-024-05692-y), and GraphPad Prism (no methods paper but de facto standard in wet-lab work) all return parameter SE / 95% Wald CIs computed from the local curvature of the residual sum of squares. None of these tools' methods papers reports a coverage study on simulated data. Prism's own documentation (https://www.graphpad.com/guides/prism/latest/curve-fitting/reg_standard_errors_and_confidence.htm) goes so far as to say SEs "do a poor job of conveying the precision of the best-fit parameter values" and recommends turning them off in routine reports.

- **Bootstrap or replicate-resampling CIs on top of NLS / spline fits.** opm (Vaas et al. 2013, ~230 citations; DOI 10.1093/bioinformatics/btt291), grofit (Kahm et al. 2010, ~500 citations; DOI 10.18637/jss.v033.i07), and QurvE (Wirth et al. 2023, ~60 citations; DOI 10.1038/s41596-023-00850-7) all expose a bootstrap-based CI option. None of the three papers reports a calibration / coverage analysis of the bootstrap intervals on simulated data — bootstrap is documented as an *available option*, not validated as a calibrated procedure for this model class.

- **Bayesian / Gaussian-process methods with credible intervals.** fitderiv / omniplate (Swain et al. 2016 Nature Communications, ~130 citations; DOI 10.1038/ncomms13766) and AMiGA (Midani et al. 2021 mSystems, ~50 citations; DOI 10.1128/mSystems.00508-21) both use Gaussian-process regression and return posterior mean ± 95% credible bands on the OD trajectory and its derivative. biogrowth (Garre et al. 2023 JSS, ~33 citations; DOI 10.18637/jss.v107.i01) wraps FME::modMCMC (Adaptive Metropolis) and returns 5–95 percentile bands from `predictMCMC`. bletl (Osthege et al. 2022, ~18 citations; DOI 10.1002/elsc.202100108) uses PyMC NUTS on a Student-t random-walk model of µ(t) together with a `calibr8` measurement-error model. **None of these four reports a frequentist coverage check of its posterior intervals**; calibration in each paper is qualitative ("intervals widen when noise is higher", or recovery of known derivatives on a single simulated example).

- **Multistart NLS with information-criterion model selection, no built-in CIs.** Kinbiont (Angaroni et al. 2025 Nature Communications, 3 citations; DOI 10.1038/s41467-025-61592-6) explicitly uses ~50-restart k-means-seeded nonlinear optimization with AICc-based model selection. Its default `kinbiont_fit` returns point estimates and loss/AIC values; no SE, CI, or posterior is exposed by default. The paper notes Bayesian / posterior estimation as a future extension. (Synthetic-data benchmarks exist for parameter recovery but not for interval coverage.)

- **Model-free / smoothed-derivative.** gcplyr (Blazanin 2024 BMC Bioinformatics, ~55 citations; DOI 10.1186/s12859-024-05817-3) deliberately avoids parametric fitting and reports point estimates from smoothed per-capita derivatives — no uncertainty by default.

If we treat citation count as a proxy for "what the field actually runs", the picture is stark. The five most-cited entries in this table are Zwietering et al. 1990 (~4,200 cites, defines the parameterizations everyone fits but supplies no uncertainty machinery), drc (~3,100), Baranyi & Roberts 1994 (~2,600), Growthcurver (~740), and grofit (~500). Three of these five give no uncertainty by default in their methods papers, and the other two give Wald-type CIs from NLS — i.e. **the dominant uncertainty workflow seen in microbial growth-curve analyses is "Levenberg-Marquardt on Gompertz/logistic/Baranyi + asymptotic Wald CI from the local Jacobian", essentially untested for coverage under model misspecification**.

## Calibration in the methods papers themselves

Of the 16 software / position papers reviewed (excluding Prism and growthrates which have no methods paper), **0 report a formal coverage check** ("does the nominal 95% interval cover the truth 95% of the time on simulated data spanning realistic parameter ranges?"). The closest are:

- **fitderiv (Swain et al. 2016)** — partial. Includes a worked example where the GP recovers the known derivative of a synthetic curve; demonstrates that credible bands grow with noise. No sweep over parameter ranges, no quantitative coverage report.
- **bletl (Osthege et al. 2022)** — partial. The `calibr8` measurement-error calibration is validated against cell-dry-weight standards; the µ(t) posterior is not separately coverage-tested.
- **Kinbiont (Angaroni et al. 2025)** — partial. Synthetic benchmarks on diauxic, ethanol-bioproduction and phage-bacteria data exist; they report parameter-recovery accuracy but no CI / coverage analysis because the default output has no CI.
- **Ghenu, Marrec & Bank 2023 (review / critique, ~20 citations; DOI 10.3389/fevo.2023.1313500)** — yes. This is the one entry in the table that actually runs simulation-based diagnostics: they show that statistical max-per-capita-rate estimators (µ_max) systematically diverge from the kinetic intrinsic rate (µ) under realistic conditions, and they audit reproducibility of published growth-curve analyses, finding that >10% of surveyed papers fail to disclose how growth rate was computed.

Otherwise, calibration / coverage of the uncertainty intervals is **not** part of the published validation of any of these tools.

## Specific quotes / paraphrases on uncertainty and calibration

- **Growthcurver (Sprouffske & Wagner 2016)** — Vignette: "the fitting algorithm provides a measure of uncertainty for each [parameter]"; `gcfit$vals` exposes `k_se, n0_se, r_se, sigma, df`. Paper itself uses "the non-linear least-squares Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm" (minpack.lm) and validates only by correlating output metrics on 937 *E. coli* wells — no simulated-truth comparison.
- **opm (Vaas et al. 2013)** — "Fitting splines rather than growth models is more robust for these data"; "Confidence intervals can be obtained via bootstrapping or aggregating pre-defined groups."
- **grofit (Kahm et al. 2010)** — "Bootstrap and cross-validation techniques are used for estimating confidence intervals of all derived parameters."
- **drc (Ritz et al. 2015)** — Wald CIs from the variance-covariance matrix; t-distribution for continuous outcomes, normal distribution for binomial/Poisson; profile-likelihood available via `profile()` on demand.
- **fitderiv / omniplate (Swain et al. 2016)** — "estimates errors both in the inference and in any summary statistics, such as lag times". Nonparametric Gaussian-process inference of the first and second time derivatives of OD.
- **AMiGA (Midani et al. 2021)** — "AMiGA models growth curves with GP regression and infers biologically meaningful microbial growth parameters … The sampling approach provides summary statistics for each of the growth parameters in terms of mean, standard deviation, and confidence interval." Default plots show "95% credible intervals, including measurement noise"; differential growth tested via Bayes factor.
- **QurvE (Wirth et al. 2023)** — Three fitting paths (parametric NLS on log-transformed data; Theil-Sen sliding window on log(OD); smoothing spline first-derivative max). "QurvE incorporates bootstrapping for spline fitting, enhancing the reliability of curve fits by estimating uncertainty, validating the model and identifying potential outliers." No coverage study.
- **biogrowth (Garre et al. 2023)** — Vignette: `predict_growth_uncertainty()` returns 5–95% and 10–90% percentile bands by Monte-Carlo sampling from user-supplied parameter distributions; `predictMCMC()` does the same from `FME::modMCMC` posteriors. NLS fits return Wald SEs but not bootstrap by default.
- **Kinbiont (Angaroni et al. 2025)** — Multistart nonlinear optimization (k-means seeding, ≥50 restarts) with AICc-based model selection across hard-coded primary models and user-supplied ODE systems; no SE/CI/posterior in default `kinbiont_fit` output. Authors note Bayesian extension as future work.
- **GrowthRates (Hall et al. 2014)** — Sliding 5-point linear regression on ln(OD); reports slope, SE, and r²; quality heuristic: "if the standard error is >3% of the growth or if the correlation coefficient is <0.995, the results may well not be reliable". No CI.
- **bletl (Osthege et al. 2022)** — "this method enables practitioners to quantify time-variable growth rate with Bayesian uncertainty quantification and automatically detect switch-points that indicate relevant metabolic changes". Internally PyMC NUTS sampling of a Student-t random-walk model of µ(t), coupled with a `calibr8` calibration model.
- **DMFit / Baranyi-Roberts** — DMFit "obtain[s] parameters estimates from data … calculate growth or death rate, lag phase time, initial or final cell count and estimate standard errors on these parameters", with 95% Wald CLs derived from the SEs.
- **Zwietering et al. 1990** — Foundational reparameterization paper for modified-Gompertz, logistic, Richards, and Schnute primary models in terms of µ_max, λ, A. No uncertainty quantification.
- **GraphPad Prism documentation** — "By default… Prism only offers the choice of reporting the standard error values if you choose to report the symmetrical confidence intervals. The default approach in Prism is to report asymptotic approximate symmetrical confidence intervals, also called Wald confidence intervals." Profile-likelihood ("asymmetrical") CIs available as a non-default option. Documentation itself warns that SEs "do a poor job of conveying the precision of the best-fit parameter values."
- **Ghenu, Marrec & Bank 2023** — "Kinetic models estimate the intrinsic growth rate µ whereas statistical methods estimate the maximum per capita growth rate µ_max", and "through math and simulations [we] show the conditions in which µ_max is not a good estimator of µ". Documents that "over 10% of the papers reviewed reported insufficient information about how growth curves were analyzed".

## Interpretation for the methodological paper

1. **Default outputs are point estimates or Wald CIs.** In nearly every tool whose methods paper has been peer-reviewed for microbial-growth fitting, the user who runs `summary()` (or the GUI equivalent) sees parameter values and at most asymptotic SE/CI from the NLS Jacobian. Only fitderiv, AMiGA, biogrowth (in MCMC mode), and bletl deliver intervals derived from a posterior rather than from a local-quadratic approximation.
2. **Calibration of these intervals against simulated truth is essentially absent in the literature.** No tool in this table reports a coverage curve. Ghenu, Marrec & Bank 2023 is the most rigorous public critique and explicitly attacks this gap — it shows the µ vs µ_max mismatch under realistic assumptions, but does not benchmark the CIs of any specific software.
3. **The "what the community actually runs" stack** — Zwietering parameterizations, NLS via Growthcurver / drc / Prism / DMFit, with Wald CIs — is therefore exactly the regime where misspecification can quietly inflate or deflate intervals without anyone in the published software pipeline having validated it.
